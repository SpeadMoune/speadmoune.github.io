SOPHGO SC7 HP75 Review: ARM-Based TPU for AI with a Focus on Image Recognition

Today we are reviewing the SOPHGO SC7 HP75 — a tensor accelerator that at first glance might seem like just another TPU for AI tasks from an obscure company trying to challenge NVIDIA in the AI accelerator market. It looks like business as usual: big ambitions, but probably no complete ecosystem, documentation, or support for popular frameworks. But is that really the case? Let’s find out.

TPU and Why Such Powerful Video Decoders?

Tensor Processing Units (TPUs) and Neural Processing Units (NPUs) represent the next step in the evolution of specialized computing devices for artificial intelligence tasks. These processors are designed with the specific requirements of machine learning and deep neural network algorithms in mind.

TPUs are optimized for working with tensors — multidimensional data arrays that form the foundation of most modern deep learning models. The key advantage of TPUs lies in the use of Matrix Multiply Units (MXUs), which perform matrix and vector multiplication operations at incredible speed. This makes them an efficient solution for training and inference of large models, such as language models or image recognition systems, where intensive matrix computation is required.

NPUs, on the other hand, represent a more flexible solution that combines the advantages of TPUs with additional capabilities for working with various types of neural networks. NPUs often contain specialized blocks for performing convolution, activation, and pooling operations — all important components of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) used in computer vision tasks. In addition, NPUs are optimized for working with different data precisions, allowing a balance between performance and energy efficiency.

The key difference between TPUs and NPUs versus GPUs lies in their ability to more efficiently perform specialized neural network tasks. While GPUs remain general-purpose computing devices capable of handling a wide range of parallel computations, TPUs and NPUs offer unmatched performance and energy efficiency in narrowly focused AI tasks.

In the case of the SC7 HP75, in addition to being a TPU rather than the familiar GPU, its video decoders stand out: they support H.264 and H.265 codecs and can process up to 2,400 frames per second at 1080p resolution. The importance of this is difficult to overstate for those working with multiple video streams simultaneously — from security systems with facial recognition to behavioral analysis in “smart” cities. Raw computing power without the ability to handle large video stream volumes is of little use, as video decoders would otherwise become the bottleneck for the entire accelerator.

SC7 HP75 Specifications

Let’s get to what makes the SC7 HP75 so attractive for neural network workloads. It is built on a 24-core ARM A53 processor running at 2.3 GHz, delivering up to 169,280 DMIPS of computational power.

In terms of compute performance, the SC7 HP75 reaches up to 96 TOPS in INT8, 48 TFLOPS in FP16/BF16, and up to 6 TFLOPS in FP32. This makes it suitable for intensive AI tasks, including training and inference of large models. The SC7 HP75’s thermal design power is a modest 75 watts, meaning even passive cooling can handle heat dissipation — the ARM architecture has delivered on its promise of high energy efficiency. On board is 48 GB of LPDDR4x memory with a bandwidth of 205 GB/s, ensuring high data throughput. Connectivity is via a PCIe Gen3 x16 interface with PCIe Gen3 x8 support.

Special attention has been paid to video processing: H.264 and H.265 video decoding up to 2,400 frames per second at 1080P resolution, with support for decoding at 8K, 4K, 1080P, 720P, and lower resolutions. Video encoding capabilities include 900 frames per second at 1080P with support for 4K and 1080P, making this accelerator optimal for handling large numbers of video streams in security systems and smart cities. JPEG image encoding can reach 1,200 images per second at 1080P resolution, with a maximum resolution of up to 32768×32768 pixels.

Support is claimed for popular AI frameworks such as TensorFlow, PyTorch, Caffe, MXNet, and ONNX, as well as Linux-based operating systems.

ARM Architecture: Energy Efficiency and Speed

The foundation of the SC7 HP75 is a 24-core ARM A53 running at 2.3 GHz. The ARM architecture has long established itself as an efficient and power-saving solution, and in the SC7 HP75 this plays an important role. The faster and more efficiently data can be processed, the more tasks can be completed per unit time, which is especially critical when working with video and real-time inference.

In addition, the ARM architecture allows the SC7 HP75 to outperform competitors such as the Nvidia T4, particularly in tasks requiring fast response and minimal latency in video stream analysis and object recognition.

Comparison with Nvidia T4

When it comes to performance, the SC7 HP75 clearly challenges the Nvidia T4. In deep learning model benchmarks such as ResNet-50, the SC7 HP75 delivers strong results: 7,082 operations per second versus 6,285 for the T4. In object detection tasks such as SSD-Large, the SC7 HP75 again leads with 149 operations versus 142 for the competitor, and 214 for the SC7 HP75 with BERT-LARGE versus 213 for the T4.

AMD CDNA 4 vs NVIDIA: A New Round of Competition

In the world of high technology, battles that can reshape an entire industry are rare. One such battle is unfolding right now between two giants — NVIDIA and AMD. At stake is control of the AI GPU market, which is valued at tens of billions of dollars in 2025.

For a long time NVIDIA seemed unbeatable in this space, controlling up to 90% of the AI accelerator market. But in recent years AMD has been intensifying its push, offering an alternative that has seriously caught the attention of major cloud players. Let’s break down what is happening in this technology war and why the outcome matters to all of us.

How the Rivalry Began

The history of this rivalry goes back to 2006, when two key events took place. NVIDIA introduced CUDA — a revolutionary technology that made it possible to use graphics processors not just for gaming but for complex computation. Around the same time, AMD acquired ATI, becoming a full-fledged competitor in the graphics space.

At first glance, the companies appeared to be on equal footing. However, CUDA proved to be a turning point. This technology allowed developers to easily program GPUs for scientific tasks, machine learning, and other complex calculations. While AMD focused on delivering affordable solutions for the mass market, NVIDIA methodically built an ecosystem for high-performance computing.

By the 2010s the gap had become obvious. NVIDIA, with its Ampere and Ada Lovelace architectures, dominated data centers, especially in neural network training. The emergence of technologies like DLSS and specialized Tensor Cores cemented the company’s leadership in AI.

AMD’s Counterattack: The Instinct Series

AMD was not about to give up without a fight. In the 2020s the company launched the Instinct processor series, specifically designed to compete with NVIDIA’s AI offerings. The MI200 and MI300 models demonstrated that AMD was capable of building competitive products, especially in terms of price-to-performance ratio.

The real breakthrough came with the release of the MI355X in 2025. This accelerator represents a serious claim to leadership in certain market segments. Built on the CDNA 4 architecture and manufactured using TSMC’s advanced 3-nanometer process, the MI355X outperforms NVIDIA’s B200/GB200 in a range of inference tasks — its performance is 20–30% higher, and in some scenarios it achieves a threefold advantage over the competition.

AMD paid particular attention to energy efficiency, though this came at the cost of power consumption. The MI355X has a rated TDP of approximately 1,400 W, noticeably higher than its predecessor the MI350X (about 1,000 W). For data center operators this increase means careful planning of cooling and power infrastructure, as well as a more considered calculation of total cost of ownership.

NVIDIA’s Response: Blackwell and Hopper Architectures

NVIDIA is not standing still. The company responded to AMD’s challenge with the release of the new Blackwell (GB200) architecture and an updated Hopper lineup (H100/H200). These processors display impressive technical specifications: up to 208 billion transistors in the GB200 and memory bandwidth exceeding 2 terabytes per second.

NVIDIA’s key advantage remains its mature CUDA ecosystem. Millions of developers worldwide use this platform, creating a powerful network effect. When a company builds an AI application on CUDA, switching to another platform requires significant investment of time and resources.

Technology Battle: Where AMD Wins and Where It Falls Short

A comparison of today’s solutions reveals an interesting picture. AMD is betting on economic efficiency and open standards, while NVIDIA focuses on maximum performance and a developed ecosystem.

The Economics of the Rivalry

The companies’ financial metrics speak clearly about the current state of affairs. NVIDIA is enjoying a true golden age thanks to the AI boom. The company’s revenue in fiscal year 2025 reached record highs, and market capitalization exceeded a trillion dollars.

AMD has chosen a different strategy. Rather than attempting to compete with NVIDIA head-on in the premium segment, the company focuses on attracting customers with more accessible prices and energy-efficient solutions. Over the past three years AMD has made nine major acquisitions, strengthening its position in AI technology.

This strategy is starting to pay off. Large cloud providers are increasingly viewing AMD solutions as a way to reduce capital and operating costs when deploying AI infrastructure.

The Battle for Cloud Services

Competition is particularly fierce in the cloud computing segment. Here AMD can offer a compelling alternative to expensive NVIDIA solutions, especially for companies that do not require maximum performance but value economic efficiency.

The integration of AMD processors with cloud platforms such as Vultr and Microsoft Azure opens new opportunities for business. Companies gain access to modern AI capabilities without the need to invest in their own infrastructure or pay a premium for excess performance.

AMD’s open software platform ROCm is also finding supporters among developers who prefer not to depend on NVIDIA’s proprietary solutions.

What This Battle Means for Everyday Users

At first glance it may seem that this battle of giants concerns only large corporations and cloud providers. However, the consequences of this rivalry affect each of us.

Competition drives innovation and lowers prices. If AMD succeeds in capturing a significant market share, this will lead to lower costs for the AI services we use daily — from voice assistants to recommendation systems in social networks.

The development of energy-efficient solutions also has important environmental significance. Data centers consume enormous amounts of electricity, and a shift to more efficient processors can substantially reduce the carbon footprint of the digital economy.

Looking Ahead

Between 2025 and 2028, AMD plans to release the MI400 processor, which should pose an even more serious challenge to NVIDIA’s dominance. The success of this product will depend greatly on AMD’s ability to scale production and attract more developers to the ROCm ecosystem.

NVIDIA, for its part, has no intention of surrendering its position. The company is investing billions of dollars in research and development, striving to maintain technological leadership. However, NVIDIA’s high product prices create a niche that AMD can effectively fill.

Experts note that AMD represents a better risk-to-reward ratio for investors. While NVIDIA trades at high multiples reflecting expectations of continued growth, AMD can offer significant upside potential at a more reasonable valuation.

Conclusion

The AMD and NVIDIA confrontation in AI technology is far from over. NVIDIA still maintains overwhelming leadership thanks to its developed ecosystem and technical superiority in key segments. However, AMD is demonstrating that an alternative approach — focusing on economic efficiency and open standards — can be successful.

For consumers and businesses, this competition means more choice, better prices, and more innovative solutions. In a world where artificial intelligence is becoming ubiquitous, healthy competition between technology giants is exactly what is needed to accelerate progress and democratize access to cutting-edge technologies.

The battle is not over, and the next few years will show whether AMD can become a genuine threat to NVIDIA’s empire or will remain a niche player in the shadow of the market leader.

Lessons from Recent Cyberattacks: The 3-2-1 Backup Principle

Imagine the morning of July 29, 2025: airports across Russia in chaos. A major Russian airline is forced to cancel dozens of flights — mostly domestic — due to a powerful cyberattack. Booking and flight management systems are paralyzed, passengers are panicking, and the company is losing millions. This is not a movie scenario but a real incident in which hackers demonstrated just how fragile IT infrastructure can be, even for aviation giants. This example shows how important it is to have a comprehensive incident response and backup plan to minimize the consequences of such attacks. Events like these serve as a reminder: in an era when data is the lifeblood of business, the absence of a reliable contingency plan can cost not only money but reputation. Such attacks are a vivid lesson underlining that without well-thought-out backup procedures, recovery becomes a marathon rather than a sprint.

Anatomy of a Failure

Let’s break down what goes wrong in cases like these. The root of the problem often lies in outdated equipment that has gone years without updates, making it an easy target for exploits. Add to this a weak Security Operations Center (SOC) that cannot respond to suspicious activity in real time. The network is not properly segmented, so malicious code spreads like wildfire, capturing everything in its path. But the main failure is in the backups. If they are stored on the same network as the primary data, hackers can easily encrypt or destroy them. As a result, recovery drags on for days or weeks: teams frantically search for surviving fragments while the business sits idle. In recent incidents, the chaos lasted hours but could have lasted longer if not for swift countermeasures — and this despite the attack not being especially sophisticated.

The 3-2-1 Principle

To avoid such pitfalls, there is a proven backup formula — 3-2-1. The essence is simple: keep three copies of data, on two different types of media, with one copy kept offsite. This creates barriers: even if hackers reach the primary system, you still have recovery options. A key lesson from recent attacks is that storing backup copies offsite and using different media types is a fundamental part of a resilience strategy. But the world evolves, and so does the formula. Consider the extended 3-2-1-1 variant: an additional copy is stored in an isolated environment inaccessible to modification. Or 4-3-2: four copies on three media types, two of which are offline. And 3-2-1-0 means zero errors in the process, with automated integrity tests of backups.

Variant Copies Media types Offsite/Offline Additional feature
3-2-1 3 2 1 offsite Basic level for small business
3-2-1-1 3 2 1 offsite + 1 isolated Ransomware protection via immutable copies
4-3-2 4 3 2 offline For large systems with geo-distribution
3-2-1-0 3 2 1 offsite Auto-tests for zero-error risk

In modern infrastructure, 3-2-1 is actively supplemented and deployed with immutable snapshot and WORM storage technologies, which is especially important for protection against modern ransomware attacks. These approaches require no genius — they are about balancing reliability and cost.

Implementation Technologies

The principle can be implemented in various ways depending on your needs. Cloud services like AWS or Azure are ideal for geo-distribution: data is replicated across data centers worldwide, ensuring availability even during a local failure. Tape drives suit long-term archiving — they are cheap, power-independent, and resistant to magnetic fields, but slow when it comes to recovery. And to combat ransomware, WORM (write once, read many) or immutable backups are indispensable: these technologies make copies unchangeable for a set period, so hackers cannot encrypt them.

The choice depends on the scenario: for large airlines, cloud solutions could have accelerated the return to normal operations.

IT Processes Matter More Than Security Tools

Many focus on expensive security tools (SIEM, DLP) but forget: a true fortress is built on everyday IT processes. Regular software updates close the gaps through which attacks penetrate. Network segmentation limits the spread of a threat — like bulkheads on a ship, preventing the entire hull from flooding. Monitoring with modern SOC and SIEM systems makes it possible to quickly detect incidents and reduce response time. Monitoring detects anomalies early, and strict access control minimizes insider risks. Backup is the cornerstone integrated into these processes, not a separate ritual. Without this foundation, even the best antivirus is like a lock on a cardboard door.

The ITGLOBAL.COM Approach

Practice shows that the first step is an audit of existing infrastructure. This reveals bottlenecks: outdated servers, weaknesses in network segmentation, underdeveloped backup scenarios. Without this stage, any improvements will be piecemeal and unsystematic.

From there, a backup strategy is built not from a template but tailored to specific business processes. The 3-2-1 standard is adapted to real conditions: in some cases it makes sense to connect cloud services for rapid replication; in others, immutable copies with change protection should be added. For industries with elevated risks — such as aviation, transportation, or finance — combining offline and offsite storage becomes mandatory.

Particular attention is paid to vulnerability verification and recovery testing. System scanning identifies security gaps in advance, while regular recovery drills reveal how long it will actually take to return to working order. This cycle — audit, remediation, verification, staff training — turns backup into a continuous process.

The result: companies receive not just a set of technologies but a well-tuned mechanism that, in the event of an incident, reduces downtime from weeks to hours.

From Concepts to Practice

For small businesses, basic 3-2-1 with cloud providers is enough — it is affordable and does not require an army of IT specialists. Medium-sized companies add immutable copies and disaster recovery plans (DRP) to simulate attacks and practice recovery. Large players, such as international airlines, go further: geo-distributed data centers, tape archives for compliance, and automated tests. The main thing is to start with a risk assessment: what does one hour of downtime cost? This helps justify the investment and move from theory to action, integrating backups into daily operations.

Conclusion: Security as a Process

In a world where cyberattacks evolve faster than defensive measures, reliable backup is the difference between paralysis lasting weeks and getting back on track in hours. Recent incidents in the aviation sector have shown: ignoring the 3-2-1 principle means playing roulette with the future of a business. Security is not static; it is a continuous process where every step — from updating to backing up — strengthens resilience. Implement it today, and tomorrow an attack will be not an ending but merely a pause.

How to Choose the Right Processors for Different Cloud Segments

Cloud infrastructure must work equally well with enterprise ERP systems, modern containerized applications, and databases. Choosing processors for such a universal platform becomes a complex task requiring consideration of performance, economic feasibility, architectural flexibility, and scalability potential.

The server processor market has evolved from the universal solutions of early Intel Xeon Scalable generations to today’s specialized 5th and 6th generation CPUs with built-in accelerators. Understanding this evolution and correctly interpreting processor specifications has become a critical skill for building cloud infrastructure.

From Universality to Specialization: The Evolution of Server CPUs

The approach to selecting processors for cloud platforms has changed radically over the past five years. Where providers once looked for universal solutions capable of handling any type of workload with acceptable efficiency, today’s market demands specialization.

The latest-generation processors have received built-in hardware accelerators for specific operations — working with in-memory databases, cryptographic computation, and data compression. This allows hardware to be matched more precisely to the workload profile and substantially optimizes the economics of a cloud platform. With the right processor and accelerator combination, a 30-40% performance boost for specific tasks can be achieved without increasing the number of cores or clock speed.

To build effective cloud infrastructure, several key processor parameters must be taken into account. Let’s look at the main selection criteria and how they affect performance across different workload types.

Base Clock Speed: The Foundation of Cloud Versatility

In cloud infrastructure, applications with fundamentally different computational requirements run simultaneously. Legacy software developed 10-15 years ago often uses one or two cores and requires maximum clock speed for acceptable performance. Modern microservice architectures, by contrast, parallelize effectively across dozens of cores.

A high processor base clock speed makes it possible to serve both workload types without compromise. Processors with a base clock of 2.5 GHz or higher are the optimal choice for a universal cloud platform. For example, the Intel Xeon Gold 6548N with a base clock of 2.8 GHz and 32 cores boosts to 4.1 GHz in Turbo Boost mode, providing effective operation both for legacy single-core applications and for modern distributed systems.

When choosing a processor, it is important to understand the difference between base and turbo clock speeds. The base clock is guaranteed for all cores under any load, while the turbo clock is only achieved under specific conditions — usually when only a portion of the cores are active. For cloud infrastructure where all cores are constantly under load, the base clock becomes more important than the maximum turbo frequency.

Core Economics: Why 32 Is the New Sweet Spot

The number of cores in a processor directly affects two key factors of cloud infrastructure: virtual machine placement density and scaling granularity. With typical resource overcommitment, a 32-core processor provides an optimal balance between virtualization density and scaling flexibility, allowing computational resources to be used effectively without excessive cost.

32-core processors represent the optimal solution for most scenarios. They provide sufficient power for enterprise workloads, a reasonable growth margin, and a comfortable level of redundancy. The capacity growth increment is approximately 25-30% of existing infrastructure, allowing the platform to scale smoothly without excess.

Built-in Accelerators: When the Suffix Says It All

Modern Intel Xeon processors use letter suffixes to denote built-in accelerators — specialized hardware blocks that significantly speed up specific operations. Understanding these designations is critical when selecting processors for a specific workload profile.

The main suffixes and their meaning:

N (Network) — optimization for network functions, including packet processing and network virtualization. Such processors show better performance in SDN tasks, virtual switches, and network function virtualization (NFV).

P (Performance) — contains Intel In-Memory Analytics Accelerator to speed up in-memory data operations. Critical for in-memory databases like Redis or SAP HANA, where data access speed determines overall system performance.

Q (Quick Assist) — hardware acceleration of cryptographic operations and data compression. Substantially reduces CPU load during SSL/TLS termination, VPN server operation, and backup operations.

V (VM Density) — optimization for maximum virtualization density with improved memory handling and reduced overhead for large numbers of virtual machines.

For a cloud provider, the optimal strategy involves infrastructure segmentation: base models with the N suffix for general-purpose workloads, specialized versions for dedicated clusters serving specific tasks.

Economic Math: The Cost Per Gigahertz

When analyzing the economic efficiency of processors, it is important to look not only at the absolute cost but also at the cost per unit of computational power. When moving between processor generations, the cost per gigahertz may remain comparable, but the quality of that gigahertz changes substantially.

5th-generation processors support DDR5 memory running at up to 5,600 MHz — nearly twice as fast as DDR4. This means that at the same processor clock speed, real-world performance of applications that work intensively with memory increases by 20-30%. The economic benefit becomes clear when calculating total cost of ownership (TCO): fewer but more powerful servers require less data center space, consume less electricity, and require less administrative effort.

Energy Efficiency and Space Savings

Modern processor manufacturing processes have fundamentally changed the approach to data center planning. Intel’s 5th-generation processors demonstrate 20-25% better performance-per-watt compared to the 3rd generation.

In practice, this means more computing power can be fit into the same server rack. Where a certain level of performance once required 10 rack units of equipment, 6-7 units now suffice. The freed space can be used for further scaling or for improving the cooling system, which is especially relevant with growing density requirements.

SST-PP and Enhanced Turbo Boost

Traditional Turbo Boost operated on a binary choice principle — either one core at maximum frequency or all cores at base speed. Modern processors use a more flexible scheme capable of simultaneously accelerating various numbers of cores depending on the nature of the workload.

This flexibility is critical because different business tasks present opposite requirements. Some, like HPC or databases, need maximum single-core frequency, while multithreaded scenarios need the maximum number of cores for parallel tasks. Tailoring hardware to each task leads to a non-unified server fleet, complicating infrastructure management and reducing flexibility.

Intel Speed Select Technology - Performance Profile (SST-PP) addresses this problem directly. It allows processor performance to be configured for current tasks without replacing hardware, switching profiles (number of active cores and their frequency) through BIOS or utilities. This simplifies management, reduces hardware costs, and provides greater flexibility in resource allocation.

Workload Segmentation: Different Processors for Different Clients

In real cloud infrastructure, a single processor type is rarely used throughout. The typical segmentation scheme is built around workload profile and business task, not a formal cloud category.

Public clouds typically use general-purpose processors like the Intel Xeon 6548N, which provide a balance between price and performance. They are suitable for most typical scenarios — web servers, container platforms, virtual desktops.

ERP systems and 1C-based solutions require fewer cores but higher clock speeds. In such cases, processors like the Intel Xeon Gold 6544Y (16 cores, 3.6 GHz) are optimal, providing high performance for single-threaded operations.

High-load databases and analytics platforms (SAP HANA, Redis, Memcached) work efficiently on processors with memory accelerators, such as the Intel Xeon 8558P. The built-in In-Memory Analytics Accelerator blocks allow data processing speed to be increased without cluster expansion.

Private clouds in this scheme are not separated into a distinct category — they can be built on any of the listed configurations depending on the nature of the client’s workload.

Real-World Scenarios

Case 1: A client needed to deploy 1C infrastructure with high transaction throughput and minimal interface latency. Testing showed that standard processors with a large number of cores and a relatively low clock speed (e.g., 32 cores at 2.8 GHz) did not deliver the needed performance in single-threaded scenarios. The solution was a configuration based on Intel Xeon Gold 6544Y processors (16 cores, 3.6 GHz / 4.1 GHz Turbo) with fewer cores but a higher base frequency. Combined with careful BIOS tuning in High Performance mode, this delivered stable 1C operation under peak loads and reduced interface response time by tens of percent.

Case 2: A second project required hosting an analytics platform based on SAP HANA, where the priority was high computational density and performance when working with RAM. The optimal choice here was Intel Xeon 8558P processors with 48 cores at 2.7 GHz and a built-in In-Memory Analytics Accelerator that reduces compression/scanning overhead and increases throughput. They provided a significant speed improvement for parallel data processing and allowed high-load systems to be placed compactly, reducing the number of servers used.

Both examples show that the choice of processor is determined not just by generation or price, but primarily by the nature of the workload. For systems with intensive single-threaded operations, core frequency matters; for analytics and in-memory solutions, core count and memory bandwidth are key.

Balancing Versatility and Efficiency

Choosing processors for cloud infrastructure is always a search for an optimal balance. On one side, universal solutions provide flexibility and ease of management. On the other, specialized models deliver substantial performance gains for specific tasks.

Intel Xeon’s 5th generation addresses this dilemma effectively. Base models remain versatile enough for any application while gaining specialized capabilities for accelerating popular scenarios. The right combination of different processor types within one infrastructure allows the full spectrum of needs to be covered — from budget solutions for startups to high-performance clusters for corporations.

Conclusion

Choosing processors for cloud infrastructure requires a deep understanding of both the technical specifications of modern CPUs and the specifics of the workloads that will run on them. The optimal solution is not the most powerful or cheapest processor but a well-matched combination of different models for specific segments of the cloud platform.

Today’s 5th-generation Intel Xeon processors offer a wide range of configurations — from universal 32-core models to specialized versions with built-in accelerators. Making proper use of this variety allows effective, scalable, and economically sound cloud infrastructure to be built that can adapt to changing market requirements.

Small Numbers, Big Possibilities: The Role of Floating Point in AI

Floating-point numbers underlie the vast majority of computer computations, especially in the fields of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning. They allow models to process data efficiently, providing a balance between accuracy and computational speed. The development of computing technology requires new formats that optimize memory usage and accelerate computational processes without significant accuracy losses. One promising format is FP8 — an 8-bit floating-point number format that can improve computational performance and reduce energy consumption.

Historical Context

Before the IEEE 754 standard (1985), working with floating-point numbers was complex and unstable. Each computing system used its own methods for handling such numbers, leading to program incompatibility and computation errors. The introduction of IEEE 754 standardized the representation of floating-point numbers, providing a unified approach to their processing. This standard defined number structure, including the sign bit, exponent, and mantissa, and established rules for rounding and handling exceptional situations.

Initially, floating-point operations were performed in software, slowing down computations. Later, specialized mathematical coprocessors appeared, such as the Intel 8087, which significantly accelerated these operations. Subsequently, this functionality was integrated into central processors, contributing to the development of high-performance computing, especially in graphics and scientific tasks.

Floating-Point Numbers in AI and Machine Learning

In machine learning, numerical precision plays an important role. Training models requires high-precision computations, since even the smallest errors in number representation can accumulate and affect the final model efficiency. However, using 32-bit and 64-bit formats demands significant computational resources, increases energy consumption, and slows data processing speed.

To address this problem, FP16 and BF16 (bfloat16) formats were developed, which reduce memory usage and accelerate computation. BF16 is a 16-bit format with an 8-bit exponent (as in FP32) and a 7-bit mantissa, allowing a wide dynamic range to be preserved at lower precision. Recent research shows that FP8 can provide an even more effective balance between precision and performance, although its practical application is currently limited to experimental implementations and specialized systems.

Precision and Quantization

Reducing the precision of number representation is one method for optimizing neural network operation. Among the most popular formats today are FP32, FP16, BF16, INT8, and the experimental FP8.

FP32 (32-bit format) provides high precision but requires significant computational power and memory.

FP16 (16-bit format) reduces hardware load but in some cases leads to precision losses due to limited dynamic range.

BF16 (brain floating-point 16) preserves the same dynamic range as FP32 thanks to its 8-bit exponent, but with lower mantissa precision. This makes it especially useful for model training where overflow avoidance is important.

INT8 (8-bit integer format) is efficient for inference but requires additional calibration to preserve model quality.

FP8 comes in two variants: E4M3 and E5M2. The first uses a 4-bit exponent and 3-bit mantissa, while the second uses a 5-bit exponent and 2-bit mantissa. E4M3 is better suited for weights and activation tensors, and E5M2 for gradients.

FP8 Features and Limitations

The E4M3 format differs from standard representations in that it does not support infinity and NaN values in order to extend the dynamic range. This creates additional complications when handling exceptional situations, requiring special emulation mechanisms in software frameworks.

It is important to note that before IEEE standardization is complete, different manufacturers use slightly different FP8 implementations. For example, AMD MI300X supports FP8 (E4M3 and E5M2) with encoding that differs from NVIDIA’s format. Similarly, the Open Compute Project defines its MX format for FP8 with minor variations.

NVIDIA research shows that FP8 approaches FP16 precision in inference, achieving comparable results with proper configuration. However, full model training in FP8 requires additional stabilization techniques: - Loss scaling to prevent gradient vanishing - Stochastic rounding to reduce error accumulation - Mixed precision, where critical operations are performed in FP16 or FP32

Hardware Support

Modern graphics processors are actively implementing support for reduced-precision formats including FP8.

NVIDIA

Hopper (H100) GPUs are equipped with 4th-generation Tensor Cores with FP8 support. The newest Blackwell architecture uses 5th-generation Tensor Cores that extend support for low-precision formats, including FP4 and FP6 in addition to FP8.

Blackwell performance: - B100 (700 W, air cooling): ~1,750 TFLOP/s in FP16 - B200 (1,000 W, air cooling): ~2,250 TFLOP/s in FP16 - B200 (1,200 W, liquid cooling): up to ~2,500 TFLOP/s in FP16

When switching to FP8, theoretical throughput can double for compute-bound tasks.

AMD

Instinct MI300X processors support native FP8 (E4M3/E5M2) operations through the ROCm 6.4.1 software stack, providing competition with NVIDIA solutions in the AI accelerator segment.

FP8 support in modern GPUs plays an important role for data centers, mobile devices, and autonomous systems. Internal tests on servers with 8×H100 show that the Llama 3 model in FP8 format with quantization consumes approximately 0.39 J/token at batch size 128.

Current State of Standardization

FP8 formats are undergoing standardization under the IEEE P3109 working group. The project was approved on February 15, 2023, but voting and ratification procedures are still ongoing.

In parallel with IEEE, the industry is not waiting for formal standardization to complete. In 2023 the Open Compute Project community introduced the MX format initiative, which includes FP8 (along with 4- and 6-bit formats) as a standard for AI accelerators. This indicates that manufacturers are already moving toward common formats (for example, NVIDIA and AMD both support E4M3/E5M2), preparing the ecosystem even before the IEEE standard is finalized.

Currently, industrial use of FP8 is primarily limited to inference tasks. Full training of large models in FP8 remains an experimental area, accessible through specialized frameworks such as NVIDIA Transformer Engine.

Practical Applications

FP8’s practical applications span several areas of modern computing. In cloud computing, reduced memory and bandwidth requirements allow larger models to be hosted on fewer accelerators, significantly reducing data center operating costs. Cloud service providers can serve more clients on existing infrastructure, improving the economic efficiency of their solutions.

For embedded systems, reduced computational requirements open new possibilities for running complex neural networks on power-constrained devices. This is especially critical for autonomous driving systems, where data from multiple sensors must be processed in real time, and for mobile applications where battery life remains an important user experience factor.

In the area of training large language models, experimental implementations demonstrate the potential to reduce training time and energy costs while preserving model quality. However, in practice this requires additional stabilization techniques and careful hyperparameter tuning, which currently limits widespread industrial adoption.

Alternative Approaches and Competitors

Alongside FP8, other methods of reducing computational precision are actively being researched, each with its own advantages in certain usage scenarios.

INT4 quantization is already used to speed up large model inference (with careful calibration to preserve accuracy) and shows particular effectiveness where system response speed is important.

Posit formats represent a fundamentally different mathematical basis for representing numbers with variable precision, potentially providing a better balance between accuracy and range of representable values. However, they remain experimental without widespread hardware support.

Logarithmic formats show promise for specific types of computation, especially where data with a wide dynamic range must be processed. Like posit formats, they are still in the research phase without practical production use.

Future Trends

The development of floating-point numbers does not stop at FP8. Research into hybrid formats combining the advantages of different number representations is ongoing. Quantum and neuromorphic computing may also influence precision requirements.

Algorithmic improvements are aimed at creating error correction methods and adaptive precision computation, which will allow low-precision formats to be used more broadly without loss of model quality.

Conclusion

FP8 represents a promising direction for AI computing, offering a combination of improved performance, reduced energy consumption, and optimal memory usage. However, the current state of the technology calls for careful evaluation: industrial application is currently limited to inference tasks, and full model training requires additional stabilization techniques.

The ongoing IEEE P3109 standardization and parallel industry initiatives such as the OCP MX format, together with expanding hardware support from leading manufacturers, point to growing technology maturity. As current technical limitations are addressed, FP8 may become an important tool for creating more efficient and energy-economical artificial intelligence systems.

What Are Artificial Intelligence Servers and Why Are They Needed?

Artificial intelligence servers are specialized computing systems for processing large volumes of data and performing complex calculations. How AI Servers Differ from Ordinary Servers

The main difference lies in the approach to computation. Both regular and AI servers use CPUs for operating system functions and task management. AI servers also differ in having multiple GPUs (typically 2-8), NVLink and PCIe 5.0 support for fast inter-card communication, larger volumes of RAM and GPU memory, and enhanced cooling systems with powerful power supplies.

GPU vs CPU: Why Graphics Cards Matter More for Machine Learning

A CPU executes diverse tasks sequentially, handling complex varied operations effectively. A GPU specializes in parallel execution of large numbers of similar operations simultaneously. According to NVIDIA, GPUs accelerate deep learning tasks up to 50 times compared to CPUs. Interestingly, graphics cards were originally created for gaming but turned out to be ideal for AI: what once rendered pixels now “draws” connections between millions of neurons.

Data Centers with Liquid Cooling: Design, Modernization, and Ready-Made Solutions

Five years ago, liquid cooling in data centers was an exotic rarity. Today it is an engineering necessity: power density is growing faster than air-based systems can handle. AI platforms reach tens of kilowatts per rack, and individual GPU chassis consume over a hundred kilowatts. Containing such thermal loads with air alone is increasingly difficult and expensive.

Air corridors and precision air conditioners no longer cope with local thermal peaks at the CPU/GPU level. Hot spots emerge, and fan energy consumption rises. Liquid removes heat closer to the source and does so more efficiently than air. But simply replacing air conditioners is not enough: questions of design, hydraulics, leak risks, and service maintenance arise. Let us examine the available approaches — direct-to-chip and immersion systems — how to modernize a hall without stopping services, and what to look for when choosing a vendor.

What Is Liquid Cooling?

Definition and Operating Principles

Liquid cooling in a data center is a system where heat from key server components is carried away by a liquid circulating in a closed loop or surrounding the equipment directly. The core idea is to remove heat as close to the source as possible and transfer it out of the rack without powerful airflows.

Why has air stopped being sufficient? Modern CPUs and GPUs have thermal design power ratings of hundreds of watts per chip. A single rack may contain 8–16 accelerators plus processors — totaling tens of kilowatts. Air must pass through dense heatsinks without allowing individual zones to overheat, which becomes technically challenging. Even when the hall temperature is comfortable, heat distribution inside the rack is uneven. Fans spin faster, increasing noise and energy consumption.

Liquid has higher heat capacity and thermal conductivity than air. It carries more heat at a lower flow volume. A cold plate on a CPU/GPU transfers heat directly to the liquid, which carries it to a heat exchanger. We cool the source, not the volume.

Advantages of Liquid Cooling

  • Reduction in PUE (down to 1.1–1.2) through lower energy consumption of air conditioners and server fans. Liquid transfers heat more efficiently, reducing the load on precision cooling systems and decreasing the share of infrastructure costs in the overall energy balance of the data center.

  • Increased placement density: more computing power can fit in the same floor space. Transitioning to direct-to-chip raises the permissible rack load without scaling the hall, which is especially important in existing data centers with space constraints.

  • Reduced noise and dust in critical zones, improving equipment stability. Lower fan speeds mean less vibration and contamination inside servers, and in immersion systems, fans are eliminated entirely.

Why Liquid Cooling Matters for Data Centers

Cooling Efficiency

In air-based systems, even at 22°C in the hall, overheating can occur inside the rack. Air cools the volume, not the source. Liquid via a cold plate removes heat directly from the chip, eliminating hot spots. This delivers stable temperature and predictable operation under peak loads.

Reduction in Energy Costs

Liquid systems save 30–50% of cooling energy compared to air systems at high density. Server fans operate at lower speeds — this yields 10–25% savings in the server’s own energy consumption.

Extended Equipment Service Life

A uniform temperature profile reduces thermal cycling and component stress. Less fan vibration means fewer mechanical failures. In immersion systems, fans are absent entirely.

Types of Liquid Cooling Systems

Direct and Indirect Cooling

Two basic architectures are distinguished in the professional field: direct-to-chip and immersion.

  • Direct-to-chip — coolant is delivered to cold plates on the CPU/GPU. The server remains in a standard form factor; part of the heat is removed by air.
  • Immersion — servers are submerged in dielectric fluid; the air circuit is eliminated.

Systems with Cooling Liquid

Direct-to-chip: cold plates are installed directly on CPUs and GPUs; the coolant circulates through distribution manifolds inside the rack; servers are connected via quick-disconnect couplings (QDCs); a CDU (Coolant Distribution Unit) maintains control over temperature, pressure, and flow. This scheme preserves the familiar server architecture and is suitable for phased modernization. Leak risks are minimized by industrial-grade hermetic QDCs, pressure and flow sensors, and negative-pressure systems (such as Chilldyne’s), where loss of seal causes air to be drawn in rather than liquid to flow out.

Immersion: servers are fully or partially submerged in dielectric fluid. In a single-phase scheme the coolant simply heats up and transfers heat through a heat exchanger; in a two-phase scheme the liquid boils on hot component surfaces and condenses back. Immersion allows operation at very high power densities but requires material, gasket, and cable compatibility with the dielectric, as well as a different service maintenance model.

Modular Solutions

Ready-made containers or racks with integrated liquid cooling are a convenient option for a pilot project or a standalone AI cluster. They connect to power and an external heat exchange system without affecting the main hall infrastructure.

Designing a Data Center with Liquid Cooling

Preparation for Installation

Before installation, it is important to address the fundamental engineering questions early. Can the floor bear the additional load — especially for immersion tanks filled with liquid? Where will the coolant be drained in an emergency, and is drainage provided? How will the mains run and where is the most convenient point to connect racks to the loop? These questions must be resolved before purchasing equipment. A common mistake is to start by selecting servers and CDUs and return to hydraulics at the last moment. The correct sequence is: thermal audit → hydraulic calculations → CDU and routing placement.

Installation Process

Installation includes: setting up the CDU, laying main pipelines and manifolds, connecting QDCs to racks, mounting temperature and pressure sensors. The ability for “hot connection” — adding racks without stopping the loop — is important. After installation, flushing and pressure testing are mandatory.

Regular Maintenance and Monitoring

The liquid loop requires systematic monitoring. Coolant quality must be checked every 3–6 months: pH, condition of corrosion inhibitors, presence of contaminants, and signs of biofouling are measured. In parallel, pressure and flow in the loop are tracked in real time — this allows rapid detection of deviations. Quick-disconnect couplings and all connection points are regularly inspected for micro-leaks. It is important not to limit activity to responding to failures: the system must record parameter trends to enable predictive maintenance and resolve issues before they develop.

How Does DDoS Protection Work?

Among the most effective methods of protection against DDoS attacks are the DDoS sensor and Multi-Layer Traffic Filtering.

A DDoS sensor (also known as a DDoS attack detector) is a device or software designed to monitor network traffic in order to detect and protect against DDoS attacks. It can operate at the level of network equipment or be implemented as software on servers. DDoS sensors use various algorithms and methods, such as traffic behavior analysis and vulnerability monitoring.

Multi-Layer Traffic Filtering is an approach to processing and analyzing traffic in computer networks that uses multiple filtering methods at different levels of the network architecture. It can include filtering at the level of network equipment, applications, protocols, and other parameters to detect and block various types of network attacks, including DDoS attacks, vulnerability scanning, phishing, and others.

Modern DDoS attacks are highly sophisticated and can combine many different attack types. Simply put, without a comprehensive DDoS protection system, the next attack may cause catastrophic damage.

The good news is that many hosting companies have begun offering DDoS protection that can help protect websites from attacks and thus avoid downtime and other security issues. DDoS protection works by carefully filtering website traffic so that illegitimate requests are not passed through while legitimate requests proceed without significant page-loading delays. Some hosting companies also offer to notify website owners of DDoS attack attempts, providing detailed records of when the attempt occurred, how large it was, and other important information.

Four Stages of DDoS Attack Prevention

DDoS attack prevention refers to the tools and techniques for protecting your networks and systems. There are four steps to successful DDoS attack prevention.

1. Detection

It is very important to detect an attack as early as possible. Even the slightest deviations in site traffic may indicate its onset.

Unfortunately, humans cannot always identify unusual behavior. Companies therefore implement User and Entity Behavior Analytics (UEBA) solutions to detect anomalous network behavior. UEBA tools use machine learning to detect deviations in the behavior not only of users but also of routers, servers, and endpoints on the network.

DDoS attack detection occurs using numerous methods, including analysis of traffic for anomalies and unusual patterns that may indicate a DDoS attack — enabling rapid response and initiation of traffic filtering to prevent malicious traffic from reaching the site or application.

2. Initial Filtering

To stop a DDoS attack once it has been identified, network filters must quickly respond by prohibiting or redirecting malicious traffic away from the intended victim. This is typically accomplished via DNS routing. Since DNS routing is always enabled, it is effective against attacks at both the application layer (L7) and the network layer (L3).

The general approach to initial filtering is based on a wide range of traffic analysis methods that allow attacking IP addresses to be isolated and blocked without causing harm to the protected servers and applications.

3. Advanced Filtering

By identifying patterns that distinguish legitimate traffic from malicious traffic, the latter can be filtered out. This can be achieved without disrupting the user experience — for example when an attack targets only one server. Often the IT department can precisely determine which traffic should be filtered by identifying which users access the affected server.

Advanced filtering involves multiple stages and uses various methods to identify and block attacking traffic. One such method is traffic behavior analysis, which allows an attack to be identified and distinguished from normal traffic by analyzing various traffic parameters such as source, volume, packets per second, protocol traffic distribution, and so on.

Machine learning-based methods are also applied to help identify new attack types that have not been previously detected. These methods are used to analyze and train models on large volumes of data, which help reveal anomalous traffic behavior. Together, they are used to identify and block DDoS attacks before they reach the client’s server. Advanced filtering can also be applied to recognize and block other types of malicious traffic, such as botnets and port scanning.

4. Post-Attack Analysis

To protect against future DDoS attacks, system administrators should analyze the attack and the security response measures. This can be done by gathering information about the attack using system logs and analytics.

After an attack has been stopped, specialists conduct a detailed analysis of the incident. This includes analyzing inbound and outbound traffic, protocols, packets, data flow, and other factors. This analysis allows the type and nature of the attack to be determined, vulnerabilities in the system that can be exploited by attackers to be identified, and a detailed incident report to be provided to clients.

Post-attack analysis also helps draw conclusions and take measures to prevent similar attacks in the future. Modern tools and technologies are used for DDoS attack analysis, including machine learning and artificial intelligence systems, enabling rapid identification of new threats and tuning of the protection system for more effective attack prevention.

In addition, detailed attack reports can be provided to clients to help them understand the nature of the attack and develop a strategy for protection against future incidents. These reports can also be used to analyze website performance and optimize DDoS protection processes.

How to Choose DDoS Protection?

Every project is individual and requires a specific approach to building protection. Below are the key questions to consider before choosing a provider.

1. Does the solution provide quality protection? Many network-level DDoS attacks can be prevented by allowing only legitimate HTTP traffic into the network. The service should block traffic unrelated to the web application, or UDP packets without payload.

2. Does the solution block all attack traffic? Not all attacks target web applications or services — sometimes they attempt to penetrate through FTP. For more effective protection, choose a service that can assess all your traffic.

3. Is the solution always on? Security controls obviously only protect a website or application when they are enabled. You need to determine the level of availability and its delivery method. Does the solution provider guarantee availability through a service level agreement?

4. Does the solution provide scalable bandwidth to handle the attack volume? A DDoS attack can flood a target in minutes with traffic it would normally receive over two years. To minimize damage, all of it must be handled. Many cloud service providers offer access to additional bandwidth needed to repel an attack when required. Clarify with your provider what peak traffic volumes they can sustain.

5. Does the solution stop attacks before they reach your data center? The goal of a cloud solution is to stop an attack before it reaches your data center. This means you do not have to worry about DDoS attacks targeting the data center directly.

6. Does the solution affect performance? E-commerce and media streaming applications require high performance. The more traffic and the more attack types, the more rules and equipment will be needed — look for a service whose architecture provides both performance and security.

7. What is the total cost? Many IT specialists look at the price of a solution but not its total cost. It is also worth considering the cost of the device, necessary backup systems, and comparing the cost of a data breach with the effectiveness of the solution.
